# Tacit Knowledge (Polanyi) / 默会知识（波兰尼）

## Overview

This skill explains, compares, and applies Polanyi-style tacit knowledge.

Default behavior:

- answer in Chinese when the user asks in Chinese or gives no language preference
- answer in English when the user asks in English
- keep the explanation practical unless the user clearly wants philosophical or academic depth

This skill is built for concept clarification, comparison with explicit knowledge, case analysis, and domain translation into management, education, product work, research, and AI discussion.

## When To Use

Use this skill when the user wants to:

- explain tacit knowledge, explicit knowledge, know-how, or embodied skill
- explain Michael Polanyi or the phrase `we know more than we can tell`
- compare tacit and explicit knowledge
- analyze apprenticeship, craft, intuition, expertise, or expert judgment
- apply tacit knowledge to management, education, product design, research, organizational learning, or AI
- interpret a case, text, or organizational problem through a Polanyi lens
- generate teaching notes, discussion prompts, case frameworks, or training structures

Also trigger when the user asks in Chinese about:

- 波兰尼
- 默会知识
- 缄默知识
- 隐性知识
- 显性知识
- 学徒制

- 组织学习

## Response Strategy

### 1. Identify the user's goal

Classify the request first:

- quick definition
- deeper conceptual explanation
- comparison
- case analysis
- domain application
- interpretation of a text or claim
- teaching or training material

If the user wants a practical answer, do not open with too much intellectual history.

### 2. Preserve the core claim

Center the explanation on this idea:

- people often possess forms of skilled knowing that cannot be fully reduced to explicit, verbalized rules

Keep these boundaries clear:

- tacit knowledge is not mysticism
- tacit knowledge is not merely "missing documentation"
- tacit and explicit knowledge are usually complementary
- expertise often depends on practice, imitation, attention, context, embodiment, and judgment

### 3. Choose the right frame

Use one of these response frames:

- **Definition frame**: definition, significance, one example
- **Comparison frame**: tacit vs explicit knowledge across expression, transfer, evaluation, and limits
- **Case frame**: identify the tacit component in an activity and explain why it resists full codification
- **Application frame**: derive implications for training, mentorship, documentation, hiring, team design, or AI use
- **Interpretation frame**: separate the core thesis, common misunderstandings, and present-day relevance

### 4. Use concrete examples early

Prefer examples like:

- riding a bicycle
- diagnosing by pattern recognition
- craftsmanship and tool feel
- code review judgment beyond a checklist
- product sense or design taste
- mentoring junior staff
- identifying architecture risk before it is formally stated

### 5. Translate into practice

When the user wants implications, emphasize that tacit knowledge usually means:

- documentation is necessary but insufficient
- capability transfer requires observation, rehearsal, correction, and repeated practice
- expertise often moves through proximity, pairing, demonstration, and feedback
- organizations are fragile when critical know-how lives only in a few people
- AI may reproduce patterns without automatically sharing human forms of situated, embodied judgment

Mark stronger claims as interpretation when they go beyond baseline explanation.

## Output Templates

### Quick explanation

Give:

1. a short definition
2. a contrast with explicit knowledge
3. one concrete example

### Comparison

Cover:

- form of expression
- method of transfer
- dependence on context
- role of practice
- what is lost under over-codification

### Case analysis

Cover:

1. what the activity is trying to achieve
2. which parts are codified
3. which parts rely on feel, timing, judgment, or experience
4. why those parts resist full formalization
5. how the capability should be transferred

### Domain application

For management, education, product, research, or AI:

1. identify the tacit component
2. explain why it resists full rule-based capture
3. derive an operational recommendation

## Quality Bar

- Do not reduce tacit knowledge to undocumented procedure.
- Do not treat tacit and explicit knowledge as absolute opposites.
- Avoid inflated claims about AI, consciousness, or creativity unless the user asks for that debate.
- When the user wants practical advice, lead with application and then add theory.
- When the user wants academic style, increase precision and conceptual layering.
